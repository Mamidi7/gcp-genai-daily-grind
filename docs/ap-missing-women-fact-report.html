Pawan Kalyan's Claim

What he said, and what the evidence shows
THE CLAIM
ICP and Sachivalayam Volunteers Involved in Women Trafficking
Pawan Kalyan, as opposition leader and Jana Sena chief, alleged that Internal Crime Prevention (ICP) workers and village/ward Sachivalayam volunteers in Andhra Pradesh were facilitating the trafficking of women and contributing to the high number of missing women cases. He made this statement after the Centre's data on 30,196 missing women was reported.
Unproven Political Statement Trafficking Allegation
🔍

What He Said

Alleged that volunteers under the Sachivalayam system were aiding human traffickers, leading to women going missing from the state.

⚖️

Consequences

AP Women's Commission issued notice seeking proof. Police registered a case for "derogatory remarks" against volunteers. YCP govt threatened legal action.

📝

Evidence Provided

As of latest reports, Pawan Kalyan had not publicly submitted concrete evidence linking ICP/volunteers to trafficking rings. Multiple media queries remain unanswered.

🔄

Subsequent Position

Later acknowledged police efforts — "Change is here, lauds police for solving missing women cases" (New Indian Express) — indicating the new govt's police reforms are showing results.

Note on ICP

ICP (Internal Crime Prevention) refers to a system of village/ward-level volunteers appointed under the YCP government's Sachivalayam system. These volunteers are responsible for data collection, welfare scheme delivery, and local grievance reporting — not law enforcement. Pawan Kalyan's claim that they are involved in trafficking remains unsubstantiated by public evidence as of May 2026. The AP Women's Commission has formally sought proof.

Key Takeaway

The 30,196 figure (Centre) and 26,000 figure (DGP) are not contradictory. The Centre's number is cumulative new reports over 3 years. The DGP's 26,000 is total active + resolved cases at a point in time. Since 23,000 were traced, only ~3,000 remain untraced — which aligns with the 3,928 + 1,730 = 5,658 active missing reported by the Home Minister (which includes both new and old unresolved cases).

Unsubstantiated Claim

Pawan Kalyan's claim that ICP volunteers are involved in trafficking remains unproven. The AP Women's Commission issued a notice seeking evidence. As of May 2026, no public evidence has been produced. This does not mean trafficking doesn't exist — 159 cases were registered in 2024 per NCRB — but the specific allegation against volunteers is not supported by publicly available evidence.

Operation TRACE

The state's primary mechanism for tracing missing women
What is Operation TRACE?

Operation TRACE (Tracking and Reuniting Abducted Children and women Efficiently) is a dedicated police operation launched by the Andhra Pradesh government to trace missing women and children. It uses a combination of database analysis, CCTV surveillance (RTGS-CCTV360), and field investigation. As of January 27, 2026, it had detected 3,159 out of 5,658 active missing persons.

Context Note

Crimes against women in AP have been declining year-over-year: from 25,503 (2022) to 22,418 (2023) to 19,952 (2024) — a 21.7% reduction over 2 years per NCRB data. However, the missing women data (30,196 over 3 years) is tracked separately by the Ministry of Home Affairs and refers to a different metric than "crimes against women."

Important Distinction

The NCRB's "Crime in India" report tracks registered crimes (cases filed under IPC/SLL). Missing persons are tracked separately through the Ministry of Home Affairs and state police records. This is why the 19,952 crime-against-women figure and the 30,196 missing women figure are NOT comparable — they measure different things.
