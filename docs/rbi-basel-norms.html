01

What are Basel Norms?

International standards for bank capital. Set by Basel Committee on Banking Supervision (BCBS).
Core Idea

Basel Norms = How much capital a bank must hold

Banks take deposits and give loans. If too many loans go bad, depositors lose money. Basel norms force banks to keep a minimum capital buffer to absorb losses before depositors are hit. Capital = Bank's own money (shareholders' funds). More capital = safer bank.
Basel Committee on Banking Supervision (BCBS)
Formed in 1974 by central bank governors of G10 countries. Meets at Bank for International Settlements (BIS) in Basel, Switzerland. India is a member. RBI implements Basel norms with India-specific adjustments.
1988 — BASEL I
First capital adequacy framework
Minimum Capital to Risk-Weighted Assets Ratio (CRAR) of 8%. Simple risk weights: 0% (govt), 20%, 50%, 100%.
2004 — BASEL II
Three pillars approach
Pillar 1: Minimum capital (credit+market+operational risk). Pillar 2: Supervisory review. Pillar 3: Market disclosure.
2010 — BASEL III
Post-crisis reforms
Stricter capital, leverage ratio, liquidity standards (LCR, NSFR), counter-cyclical buffer. Phased in gradually.
2017 — BASEL III FINAL
Output floor & standardized approach
Output floor of 72.5% on internal models. Constraints on using banks' own risk models. Fully effective 2028.

What counts as "Capital"?

Two Tiers — Core vs Supplementary

Tier 1 (Core Capital): Bank's highest quality capital. Can absorb losses while bank is operating (going concern).
  • CET1 (Common Equity Tier 1): Paid-up equity shares, retained earnings, reserves. BEST quality.
  • Additional Tier 1 (AT1): Perpetual non-cumulative preference shares, PNCPS. Can be written off or converted to equity.
Tier 2 (Supplementary Capital): Can absorb losses only if bank is being wound up (gone concern).
  • Revaluation reserves, general provisions, subordinated debt with min 5-year maturity.
  • Lower quality than Tier 1. Limited to 100% of Tier 1.
Phase 2 Exam Tip
In descriptive answers, always write CAR formula first, then explain Tier 1 vs Tier 2 with examples. Mention RBI's 9% vs Basel's 8%. This shows depth.
03

Risk-Weighted Assets (RWA)

Not all loans are equally risky. Risk weights assign danger levels to each asset type.
How it works

RWA = Loan Amount x Risk Weight

A Rs 100 crore loan to a corporate carries more risk than Rs 100 crore in government bonds. So the corporate loan gets a higher risk weight (100%) while govt bonds get 0% or 20%. Higher RWA needs more capital backing.
Asset Type Risk Weight Why?
Cash, Govt securities (sovereign) 0% Safest. Sovereign can print money to pay.
Loans to banks (AAA rated) 20% Very low default risk.
Housing loans (LTV < 75%) 50% Collateral backed. Lower risk.
Commercial real estate, corporate loans 100% Standard weight. Full risk.
Below investment grade / unrated 150% Higher probability of default.
NBFC loans (specific categories) 100-150% RBI adjusts based on category.
Key point: Risk weights can change. During COVID, RBI reduced risk weights for certain loans to ease capital pressure. Always check the latest RBI circular for current weights.
04

Basel III Additions

Beyond CAR — leverage ratio, liquidity, buffers.
3%
Leverage Ratio

Tier 1 Capital / Total Exposure. No risk-weighting. Catches banks that game RWA system. Minimum 3% globally; RBI mandated 4% for Indian banks.

2.5%
Capital Conservation Buffer

Extra CET1 above minimum. If breached, bank faces restrictions on dividends/bonuses. Builds cushion before crisis hits.

0-2.5%
Counter-Cyclical Buffer

RBI can raise this 0-2.5% during credit boom. Forces banks to build capital when times are good. Released during stress.

100%
LCR — Liquidity Coverage Ratio

High Quality Liquid Assets / Net Cash Outflows over 30 days. Must be >= 100%. Ensures bank can survive 30-day stress.

100%
NSFR — Net Stable Funding Ratio

Available Stable Funding / Required Stable Funding. Must be >= 100%. Prevents over-reliance on short-term borrowing.

Numerical Question Alert
If a bank has Rs 10,000 crore total exposure and Rs 400 crore Tier 1 capital, what is its leverage ratio? Answer: 400/10,000 = 4%. If RBI minimum is 4%, it just meets. If Basel minimum is 3%, it exceeds. These numerical traps are common.

India-Specific Rules

RBI goes stricter than Basel in many areas.

Remember: RBI always asks "What is the minimum capital adequacy as per RBI norms?" — answer is 9%, not 8%. Many students write 8% and lose marks.

Phase 2 Answer Template

If a 15-mark descriptive question comes on Basel norms, this is the structure.
15-Mark Answer Structure

How to write a scoring Basel answer

Introduction (2 marks):

"Basel norms are international capital adequacy standards set by BCBS at BIS, Basel. India adopted them through RBI guidelines under Banking Regulation Act, 1949. RBI's norms are stricter than Basel minimum in several areas."

Body — Part 1: Evolution (3 marks):

Basel I (1988) — simple 8% ratio. Basel II (2004) — three pillars. Basel III (2010) — post-crisis reforms with liquidity and leverage. Basel III Final (2017) — output floor.

Body — Part 2: Capital Structure (4 marks):

Explain CAR formula. Tier 1 (CET1 + AT1) vs Tier 2. Give numbers: RBI 9%, Basel 8%, CET1 4.5%, Tier 1 6%. Mention AT1 bonds can be written off (Yes Bank case).

Body — Part 3: Basel III Additions (3 marks):

Leverage ratio (3% global, 4% India), Capital Conservation Buffer 2.5%, Counter-cyclical buffer, LCR, NSFR.

Conclusion (2 marks):

"Basel norms strengthen banking system but excessive capital requirements can constrain credit growth. RBI's calibrated approach — stricter norms with phased implementation — balances stability and growth."

Diagram: Draw the capital structure pyramid (CET1 at bottom, Tier 2 at top). Labeled diagram = +1 mark.

Real example: Yes Bank AT1 bonds were written off in March 2020. This is a live case study. In Phase 2, mentioning recent examples shows awareness and fetches extra marks.
